He was clever and managed everything by himself—building his house, cooking, mending his clothes, and doing all chores. One afternoon, he decided to go to the village miles away to buy a new ax. His old one was worn down, and he needed a replacement. Though it was getting late and the woods grew dark quickly, he wasn’t concerned. He knew the way and thought he could find his way home in the dark. After buying the ax, the woodman set off but soon realized the road didn’t look familiar. He was deep in an unfamiliar part of the forest with trees so thick he could hardly move. As he searched for the path, he came upon six large eggs at the base of a beech tree. They were unlike anything he had ever seen, and his curiosity grew. “I’ll take these home and hatch them,” he thought. “Maybe they’ll be hens, and I’ll have eggs every day.” He picked up the eggs and found the road again. Back home, he thought about eating them but decided it might be a waste. What if one hatched into a hen that could lay more eggs? So, he decided to hatch them all. He cut up a piece of red flannel meant for shirts and wrapped each egg in fabric, placing them by the fire. For three weeks, he tended to them, making sure the fire never went out. It was a big responsibility, but he was determined. At last, the eggs began to crack, and out came tiny, dark purple hands—strange fists stretching from the shells. Six bizarre creatures, resembling goblins, sprawled on the floor, tumbling over each other like puppies. The woodman was confused. He had never seen anything like this before. The little creatures seemed cold and hungry, so he cut small holes in the fabric and draped it over them, leaving their heads sticking out. He didn’t have feeding bottles, but he dipped bits of flannel into milk and let the goblins suck on them. They grew quickly and became more mischievous, knocking things over, pulling the cat’s tail, and spreading honey on the woodman’s books. They nearly set the house on fire several times. Exhausted, the woodman thought, “When they grow up, they’ll help me. They’ll run errands and chop wood.” But they only caused more trouble. Finally, the woodman decided to get rid of them. Every evening, when the candle was lit, the goblins stopped their pranks and sat quietly around the table, staring at the flame with wide, hypnotic eyes. It was then the woodman had an idea. “I’ll take them back to the beech tree. If I put the candle in the lantern, they’ll follow it, and I can leave them there.” He placed the candle in the lantern, and the goblins crowded around, eyes glued to the flame. They followed him, stumbling through the dark forest. After a while, they reached the beech tree, and he hung the lantern on a branch. But when he turned to leave, he found himself lost. The lantern’s light was far behind, and he couldn’t see. He stumbled through the darkness, falling and feeling the branches. A branch caught his belt, and he thought it was a monster, but it was just a branch. He sat, catching his breath. Looking back, he saw the goblins sitting in a circle around the lantern, still staring at the light. Despite his anger, he found himself drawn back to it. Slowly, he crept back to the goblins. As he sat with them, it felt like a spell had been cast. The light from the lantern had a calming effect, and the goblins, once mischievous, were peaceful. The woodman, too, joined them in silence, staring into the warm, magical light of the lantern. The End~ Quiz ?Where does the woodman live at the beginning of the story? Four hundred and fifty years ago, Captain Hendrick Vanderdecken, a sailor from Amsterdam, embarked on a journey to Batavia aboard the Flying Dutchman. As they rounded the Cape of Good Hope, a fierce storm struck. The crew, terrified, begged the captain to turn back, but Vanderdecken, confident in his skills, refused. “Sail on,” he commanded, believing the storm could be weathered. Despite the storm’s fury and pleas from his crew, he shouted, “We will sail until doomsday!” Legend has it that after Vanderdecken’s defiant cry, the ship vanished, and neither he nor his crew was ever seen again. Some believe the captain was cursed, forced to sail the seas for eternity. Sailors who have glimpsed the Flying Dutchman are said to meet their doom. Two hundred years later, a British ship saw the phantom vessel, its black masts and blood-red sails shrouded in storm clouds. As it drew closer, the ghost ship vanished into the mist. A few years later, two more sailors claimed to see it near the Cape, and one soon fell to his death. Throughout the nineteenth century, reports continued. Beachgoers described the ship in vivid detail, despite never having seen a picture of it. Lighthouse keepers told similar stories, and many who claimed to have seen the Flying Dutchman later perished at sea. Whether legend or truth, the curse of the Flying Dutchman endures, with whispers of the ghostly captain and his doomed ship haunting the oceans. One day, to escape his wife’s anger, Rip wandered deep into the Catskill Mountains to hunt. As evening approached, he heard his name called and encountered a strange old man dressed in antique Dutch clothing, carrying a heavy keg. Jacobs The wind howled outside Laburnam Villa, but inside, the fire’s warmth and a chess game filled the evening with calm. Mr. White and his son Herbert played while Mrs. White knitted quietly by the fire. “Hark at the wind,” Mr. White muttered, trying to distract Herbert after realizing his king was cornered. “Check,” Herbert replied grimly. “I doubt he’ll come tonight,” Mr. White added. “Mate,” Herbert said, ending the game. As Mr. White complained about their home’s isolation, the gate suddenly banged. Soon, Sergeant-Major Morris entered—a burly man with a red face. After sharing drinks and stories of India, Morris produced a strange object: a dried monkey’s paw. “It’s magic,” Morris explained. “An old fakir placed a spell on it. It grants three wishes, but interfering with fate brings terrible consequences.” The Whites asked if Morris had used it. He admitted he had but refused to explain his wishes, only revealing that the first owner’s final wish had been for death. “You’re better off burning it,” Morris said, tossing the paw into the fire. Mr. White quickly grabbed it, fascinated. Morris reluctantly explained how to use it: “Hold it in your right hand and wish aloud—but be careful.” Joking nervously, Herbert suggested wishing for two hundred pounds. Half amused, Mr. White held up the paw and said, “I wish for two hundred pounds.” The paw twisted violently in his hand. Startled, Mr. White dropped it. Though they laughed uneasily, a sense of dread remained. The next morning, a stranger arrived from Herbert’s workplace. With a grave expression, he explained that Herbert had been caught in machinery and killed. He handed the Whites a check for two hundred pounds as compensation. Days passed in silence and grief. One night, Mrs. White suddenly remembered the monkey’s paw and begged her husband to wish their son back to life. Though terrified, Mr. White made the second wish. Soon after, a knocking began at the door. Mrs. White rushed to open it, convinced Herbert had returned. Mr. White, horrified by what might stand outside, made his final wish. The knocking stopped. Munro) “My aunt will be down presently, Mr. Nuttel,” said the very intelligent fifteen-year-old girl; “in the meantime, I will keep you company.” Framton Nuttel endeavoured to say the correct something which should duly flatter the niece of the moment without unduly discounting the aunt that was to come. Privately he doubted more than ever whether these formal visits on a succession of total strangers would do much towards helping the nerve cure which he was supposed to be undergoing. “I know how it will be," his sister had said when he was preparing to migrate to this rural retreat; "you will bury yourself down there and not speak to a living soul, and your nerves will be worse than ever from moping. I shall just give you letters of introduction to all the people I know there. Some of them, as far as I can remember, were quite nice.” Framton wondered whether Mrs. Sappleton, the lady to whom he was presenting one of the letters of introduction came into the nice division. “Do you know many of the people round here?" asked the niece, when she judged that they had had sufficient silent communion. “Hardly a soul," said Framton. “My sister was staying here, at the rectory, you know, some four years ago, and she gave me letters of introduction to some of the people here.” He made the last statement in a tone of distinct regret. “Then you know practically nothing about my aunt?” pursued the self-possessed young lady. “Only her name and address,” admitted the caller. He was wondering whether Mrs. Sappleton was in the married or widowed state. An undefinable something about the room seemed to suggest masculine habitation. “Her great tragedy happened just three years ago,” said the child; "that would be since your sister's time.” “Her tragedy?” asked Framton; somehow in this restful country spot tragedies seemed out of place. “You may wonder why we keep that window wide open on an October afternoon," said the niece, indicating a large French window that opened on to a lawn. “It is quite warm for the time of the year," said Framton; "but has that window got anything to do with the tragedy?” “Out through that window, three years ago to a day, her husband and her two young brothers went off for their day's shooting. They never came back. In crossing the moor to their favourite snipe-shooting ground they were all three engulfed in a treacherous piece of bog. He married her at once, and thought no man so fortunate as he was. They lived happily together, but, though he wished people to think otherwise, he was fonder of the golden arm than of all his wife's gifts besides. At last she died. The husband put on the blackest black, and pulled the longest face at the funeral; but for all that he got up in the middle of the night, dug up the body, and cut off the golden arm. He hurried home to hide his treasure, and thought no one would know. The following night he put the golden arm under his pillow, and was just falling asleep, when the ghost of his dead wife glided into the room. Stalking up to the bedside it drew the curtain, and looked at him reproachfully.
