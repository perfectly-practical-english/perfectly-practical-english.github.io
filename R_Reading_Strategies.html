Anger Fear |Celebration and triumph| Warning ~Ozymandias Analysis and Interpretation Form and Structure "Ozymandias" is a sonnet, which is a short poem with 14 lines. It mostly follows a rhythm called iambic pentameter, but the rhythm is not strict. The rhyme pattern is unusual, and the poem does not follow the normal two-part sonnet structure. Main Theme The main idea of "Ozymandias" is that all powerful rulers and empires will one day fall. Even leaders who believe they are great and powerful cannot stop time and decay. Their pride disappears, but the ruins remain. "Ozymandias" is a Greek version of part of the throne name of Ramesses II, an ancient Egyptian king. The poem is based on an old description of a statue. The message on the statue means something like: "I am the greatest king. If you want to see how powerful I was, look at my great works." Historical Background The poem may have been inspired by news of a huge statue of Ramesses II coming to London. The statue was brought to Britain for the British Museum in the early 1800s. Shelley wrote the poem before the statue arrived, but people were already talking about it. Other countries, including France, had also wanted the statue but failed to get it.
