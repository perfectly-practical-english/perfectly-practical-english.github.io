~Method Statement BCC - IEP - LS3 Method Statement for Applicants 19 July 2024 This document includes information about the BCC-IEP LS3 curriculum and day-to-day classroom operations that teachers and administrators need to know or want to ask about.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~General Information General Information about LS3 IEP stands for Intensive English Program. IEP is not only about “English”. It is about being “intensive”, which implies a certain amount of rigor; and about being a “program”, which implies a certain amount of standardization regarding what activities are done in every class. When students come in to LS3 as seventh-graders, they are halfway through their time at BCC. When they leave LS3 as ninth-graders, on their way to LS4, they are only three years from graduation, and three-fourths of the way through their time at BCC. So, students have a lot to learn in LS3 to prepare them for LS4. Each IEP class represents about a third of an original homeroom class of roughly 50 students. IEP class sizes are usually between 14 and 16 students. Students in the classes are screened according to their overall Grade Point Average. So, stronger classes can do most of what is required of them, while the weaker classes must work harder to achieve established goals. LS3 teachers need to cover reading, writing, listening, speaking, grammar, and vocabulary. Even though units last for three weeks, there is still a lot to cover in that time. With six different aspects of English to specifically teach and assess, formal formative assessment would be overwhelming for students and teachers. Formative assessment, therefore, is in the teachers’ hands, and needs to be done expediently during regular activities, and even the summative assessments themselves are best presented as additional learning opportunities. The curriculum has been streamlined as much as possible. The standardized activities represent the baseline of work that must be completed, while still allowing enough time for teachers to do their own special activities with their classes. However, teachers must understand that cancellations, holidays, school activities, etc. will require them to give first priority to the standardized curriculum activities.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Overview of Lessons Overview of Lessons for Each Unit Students in our program have five IEP lessons per week. There is a double period about twice a week. Ten units are covered per year, and units are three weeks long. A recommended schedule for each three-week unit is as follows: • Week 1: Four lessons from the course book, and one lesson to complete the unit grammar assessment. • Week 2: Vocabulary presentation, reading/listening text presentation, reading assessment, vocabulary review, vocabulary assessment, and an introduction to the writing assignment if time permits. • Week 3: At least three writing lessons; one for the topic introduction and class discussion, one for planning, and one for the final draft. Then, two lessons might be available for speaking or enrichment activities, although there is likelihood that various class cancellations may occur, taking up some part of the total three-week unit. Note: After all tests, parents would like to know how their children performed because it will affect their grades and final GPA. As a result, teachers need to notify their students of the results of their tests. And therefore, make sure that MS Forms are set to display scores after they are completed.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~The Course Book Week 1, Lessons 1 - 4 - Lessons of Work from the Course Book. Parents and administrators expect for our program to use a traditional course book. There are 14 units in the course book, and there is only enough time during the school year to complete 10 units. There are six to eight pages in each unit, but with other priorities, there is only enough time to do four pages from each unit. Our program does not purchase the workbook, because there is simply not enough time to do all the material that the publisher sells. The course book includes activities for reading, writing, listening, speaking, grammar, and vocabulary, but the only thing course books do well is cover grammar. All the content in the course book is available for teachers to use, but teachers are not under obligation to do the pronunciation drills, listening, speaking, and writing activities that come from the book. LS3 has other standardized activities to cover those important aspects of the curriculum. Most course books are not set up well for processing scores for grades. Course books usually have a random number of exercises for each activity. For example, one activity has three exercises, while another activity has six; and the teacher is left looking for one more exercise to create a score out of a possible 10. Yes, the teacher could add an additional exercise, but at a certain point, one wonders why we use a course book if we have to create our own exercises anyway. So, to solve the problem, LS3 considers each course book unit of four pages to be worth 40 points. That way, a teacher can mark the books and score the work subtractively, deducting a point for each response that is incorrect, insufficient, or left incomplete from the total of 40. And, of course, there are usually fewer than 40 exercises for each unit, meaning that students are normally at an advantage to end up with a few extra points. This approach imposes objectivity upon what would otherwise be a more subjective participation grade.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Content Presentation General Statement Regarding Presentation of Various Content All the pieces and parts that go into making the exercise serve as an example for developing a deeper understanding of English. An English exercise is about studying an example, not just getting the right answer, filling in the blank, memorizing a formula, or recognizing a pattern. Exercises are about taking everything apart - context, grammar, and vocabulary - and analyzing it, and interacting appropriately with the content to demonstrate that newfound understanding. Notice that some students are too hasty in completing their work in class, and that whenever students work completely on their own, they make a lot of unnecessary mistakes. Part of the strategy of doing work together is to slow the students down, so that they do the exercises with more thought and care. It is probably best for the teacher to present all content, even assessment content, as a lesson and learning experience. Most students do not really need a test as much as they need an additional lesson. Please refer to A Recommended Approach for Presenting Grammar Exercises, mentioned below regarding discussion of context, vocabulary, and peripheral grammar, even in addition to the lesson topic or assessment topic at hand. After a brief discussion of each exercise, without giving answers away, students should be prompted to select their response and then move on to the next exercise.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Grammar Presentation A Recommended Approach for Presenting Grammar Exercises With each exercise, there are plenty of relevant points to discuss, regarding context, vocabulary, and peripheral grammar, even in addition to the grammar point at hand. After a brief discussion of each exercise, without giving answers away, students are prompted to select their response and move on to the next exercise. 1. Call on students to read the exercises aloud to gain reading and speaking practice, and when they get to a “blank’, they read “blank”, so that the answer is not given away, and all students have an opportunity to respond. 2. Discuss the context. “What is the situation? What parties are involved? Who is speaking? What action is being discussed? What is the speaker thinking? What is the listener expected to think as a result? What is the intended outcome of this instance of communication?” 3. Then discuss relevant grammar. “What is the subject of the sentence? What is the action going on in the sentence? What is the main verb? What form does the main verb take? Is there a helping verb? What tense is this? Is subject-verb agreement important in this situation? Is there an object of the verb? Is this a statement or a question? If this is a question, what changes in word order would we expect to see? Is there more than one right answer? Is there anything else that we should notice or discuss about peripheral grammar?” 4. Then discuss key words. “Are there any words, definitions, related words, synonyms, antonyms, prefixes, suffixes, denotations, connotations, or usage that we can discuss?” 5. After a brief discussion, students are prompted to select their response and move on to the next exercise.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Grammar Assessment Week 1, Lesson 5 - Grammar Assessment The unit grammar assessment is performed online with MS Forms and is scored automatically. The assessment task is only 10 exercises, which represent the main points of grammar covered in the unit. Although 20 exercises would seem preferable, there is only so much time available during lesson periods. A 10-exercise format gives students enough time to carefully read and understand each exercise, and still complete the assessment before the lesson period ends. Having 10 exercises also makes it easier for students to come in for a break or lunch to make up missed work and still have time to eat something. IEP LS3 has separate standardized material to supplement for this perceived problem. The first lesson for the second week should be to introduce the unit vocabulary. The Learning Journal vocabulary list for each unit has been extracted from the World Book Encyclopedia text for that unit. Since the vocabulary comes from the reading text, pre-teaching the vocabulary helps students understand the text better. And going over the reading text reinforces the vocabulary in context. Instead of definitions, synonyms have been used to convey the meaning of the words on the vocabulary list. The synonyms are intended to build the students’ vocabulary. The idea is to have the vocabulary list benefit every student, whether their English skills are weak or strong. Students who might know all the words on the vocabulary list can benefit from studying the synonyms. Students who already know all of the words and synonyms are encouraged to develop conscious connections between the vocabulary words and the synonyms, so that when they are writing, they can pick the best words for the situation, or they can select alternative words for creating variety in their writing. Vocabulary presentations in class should involve the unit vocabulary words, the parts of speech they represent, definitions, related words, synonyms, antonyms, prefixes, suffixes, denotations, connotations, and contexts for use, and not just visual examples. Good example sentences are very important for helping students understand new vocabulary in context, and for creating cognitive hooks to help students remember words they have learned. It must be understood that a good example sentence is more than simply using a word in a sentence according to the part of speech it represents. Example sentences should contain contexts, implicit definitions, or synonyms relevant to the word, and useful to help the student understand, internalize, remember, and use the word again later. Sometimes, more than one sentence will be needed to achieve all the criteria for what a good example sentence should include. A noun should be identified by using another noun (S+LV+SC noun) or described with an adjective (S+LV+SC adjective) often used to characterize that noun. Otherwise, a noun should be presented as doing the action of a familiar verb, which a noun of that sort would normally do. A verb should be presented as being done by a familiar noun that is normally associated with doing that action. An adjective should be presented as describing a noun that normally exhibits the characteristics of that adjective. An adverb should be used in a context relevant to describing the action of a verb that a particular noun normally performs in the particular way described by that adverb (adverbs of manner); otherwise, the adverb must be relevant to the context by explaining how, why, or when the action is occurring.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Useful Outline Feature in PowerPoint Suggestion to Use the Outline Feature in MS PowerPoint Teachers should set up their vocabulary PowerPoints to employ the Outline feature in PowerPoint. LS3 texts are excerpts from World Book Encyclopedia. LS3 has purchased a license and has obtained permission to use World Book content the way it does. The recommendation is to certainly give students plenty of opportunities to practice reading out loud in class. Have students read instructions, exercises, and various snippets. But when it comes to sizeable texts, the teacher should read to the students, because reading habits must be modeled for students. Also, keeping the continuity of the text, from start to finish, is essential for comprehension. It is important for the teacher to discuss context, concepts, grammar, and vocabulary, and this can be accomplished with greater continuity if the teacher is the one reading aloud. Having the teacher read aloud to the students enables the teacher to pause and make points about the text while preserving the continuity as much as possible. Such pauses, of course, are necessary to explain context or concepts, define new words, point out synonyms, prefixes, suffixes, or interesting grammar points. Furthermore, having students read lines of text is inefficient for time management, and the continuity of the textual content is degraded whenever students read slowly, inaudibly, or with poor pronunciation. Then, the teacher further breaks the continuity by pausing to correct students, or to make points about the text. Quite often, the teacher has to read the text again anyway. So, in the end, having students read individual exercises aloud is at least as good as having students read longer texts, if not better. In lieu of reading longer texts aloud, students can practice pronunciation by simply moving their mouths as they read. This is a strategy that should be emphasized. It helps pronunciation by building muscle memory, it keeps students attentive and engaged, and it helps students make the most of any lesson, whether the lesson is hard or easy; interesting or boring. Making the most of every learning opportunity is what separates weak learners from strong learners. Double back and reread. Back up if you get lost. Read once. Get the main idea from words you know. 5. Scan titles, pictures, and captions. 6. Identify known words (+). What research shows is that although students that read a lot will eventually stumble upon a number of useful strategies, what actually helps most is when teachers model reading for students, and instruct them carefully about specific strategies. Then, reading ability improves exponentially. The reason for using third-party texts is that LS3 had already been using the EIM texts for a few years, but administrators still wanted to see improvement in students’ reading skills. At the time, some teachers thought the EIM texts were sufficient, even actually mistaking them for “authentic texts”. Other teachers expressed interest in creating our own texts, but we had already been doing that for assessments, and that had not been working out well either. During down time, for example, whenever we upgraded curriculum, one teacher would take a text and make it more difficult. Then the next year another teacher would come along and try to make it easier again. Whenever we changed course books, teachers would go through the IEP server to find old texts that matched the new topics. Sometimes they would take a Grade 9 text and give it to Grade 7, or a Grade 7 text and give it to Grade 9. Grade 9 would get off easy, while Grade 7 would struggle. Then at the end of the year, the Grade 7 teachers would complain that their students did not do well on a certain test and that we needed to fix it. Instead of adapting the text, teachers would make the questions easier; so easy in fact, that students could take the test without even reading the text. It was clear that we had to take a different approach. So, I decided that we were going with third-party texts, and teachers would only create comprehension questions for assessment. Teachers were skeptical at first, but it worked out very well. The writing plan should be considered a separate assignment from the final draft. The purpose of the writing plan template is to make students accountable for the planning they do, and to get their ideas down on paper quickly so teachers can more effectively coach students with their writing. We must stop accepting freewriting as a final draft, and we must stop students from using precious planning time to procrastinate. Using the writing plan template requires a lot of hard work from the teachers and the students. The template includes everything a reader might expect, and everything a teacher needs to see to assist a student before the fate of his grade is sealed with his final draft. - The writing template represents the thinking that needs to be done, and the concepts that must be gathered together even before a first draft is begun. - If students think that it is possible to just jump straight into the writing without doing the writing plan template at all, then doing the writing plan template should be easy. Otherwise, if the writing plan template seems too hard, students can rest assured that they will get better at it as time goes by, and that we expect to see their writing (as the result of improved planning) get better and better. - Teachers need to see students’ initial responses, main ideas, words they plan to use, and a plan for structure while students are still working. Otherwise, the students will more or less be working in a vacuum until they turn in the final product, by which time it is really too late to make any improvements. The writing plan template enables a teacher to see how a student is doing and provide feedback before the writing is turned in, at which time it is too late for feedback to do any good. - The writing plan template also enables students to get feedback from each other. - The writing plan template also enables students to prepare for enhanced classroom discussions and impromptu presentations. - LS3 foregoes the traditional midterm/final exam rituals to have more quality teaching and learning time, and to put less pressure on students. Students should understand and respect this, and they should willingly sit down to focus on important work when required to do so. - The long-range goal is to teach students how to pull ideas together quickly, and how to create a competent text efficiently. - After a few writing tasks, we should see noticeable progress in planning ability, as well as improved writing.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Writing Plan Template Instructions Instructions for the Writing Plan Template There is no strict order to fill in the information on the template. However, it is arranged with assumptions made about a sensible order for filling in the details. Basically, the top part of the template is for collecting ideas and information, and the bottom part is for organizing it. However, if students run out of ideas before completing the top part, they can go ahead and begin to organize what ideas they already have. (The idea is for students to maintain productivity and keep their pen moving.) The Title The two-part title encourages students to be more thoughtful about the title they choose, and not simply use the name of the unit assignment as a title. In the end, the title should come out more like an academic title, which represents the topic, as well as a hint about what will be said about it. After all, a title can and should be used as a context clue for the reader. For example, a title might be something like “Social Media: The Pros and Cons.” The First Column The first column is for students to simply respond to the prompts, which represent what readers want to know about the topic. Responding to such prompts is probably the most intuitive way to get started, and the template has been arranged to allow students to work that way. Strategies for the First Column - Pick the low-hanging fruit. In other words, quick-start your essay by providing the most obvious information first, and dealing with the most important and obvious issues. - Responses can be off the top of your head or straight from your gut. - If there is a prompt that requires extra thought or research, you can skip it for now, and come back to it later. - Your initial responses on the template do not have to be perfect. They can always be upgraded later. The Second Column The second column is for adding detail, and any extra or important ideas the student wants to include, contribute, or express, including their own original ideas. We cannot provide prompts for this information, but the reader expects it anyway. This section is especially useful for including extra information that the generic prompts do not cover. Strategies for the Second Column - Elaborate and add details to your responses to the prompts. - You can react to your own initial responses to the prompts according to your own knowledge, personal experience, or original ideas. - Think of ideas or information that you think should be included, but which were not elicited by the prompts. - Rephrase your initial responses to the prompts another way with other words as a means of providing context clues for your readers. - Try to cleverly incorporate your own ideas into the essay alongside straightforward responses to prompts in a way that seems logical. The Third Column The third column is for creating lists of various parts of speech; nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs. The purpose of this section is to encourage students to list and explore new words. Completing this section enables teachers to help students learn new words and use them correctly. Strategies for the Third Column - Word selection is important. - Remember that individual words can represent complete concepts. - If you cannot think of any words you want to use, identify key words that you already used in the first and second columns. - Use verbs, adjectives, and adverbs from the lists of 100 most used verbs, adjectives, and adverbs from the Learning Journal in the plastic book. - Use signal words from the Learning Journal in the plastic book, many of which are often adverbs or adverbials. - Use words from the unit vocabulary list. - List Thai words that you could use, look them up in English, and include these new words in your lists.~ BLANK ?BLANK ~Outlines and Mind Maps The Outline/Mind Map Section The spaces with “prompts” shown at the bottom are to help students organize their earlier responses, ideas, details, and words according to a universally accepted outline.
