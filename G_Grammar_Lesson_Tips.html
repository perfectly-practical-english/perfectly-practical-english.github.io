~ Common EFL Errors When teaching English as a Foreign Language (EFL), students commonly struggle with a range of language areas. These errors often reflect the differences between English and the student's first language (L1), but there are several consistent patterns across learners. The main areas of concern typically include: 1. Verb Conjugation and Tense Usage • Common errors: • Incorrect tense usage: "Yesterday I go to the store." • Third-person singular errors: "She go to school." • Overgeneralization of rules: "He goed" instead of "he went" • Why it matters: English has a complex tense system, and many languages don't distinguish time in the same way. 2. Sentence Structure and Word Order • Common errors: • Misordering: "I only eat vegetables sometimes." → "I eat only vegetables sometimes." • Transferring L1 structure directly: e.g., German or Japanese students might place verbs at the end. • Why it matters: English typically follows SVO (Subject-Verb-Object), which can differ from other languages. 3. Articles (a, an, the) • Common errors: • Omitting articles: "I saw cat in garden." • Using incorrect ones: "She is a best player." • Why it matters: Many languages lack articles, or use them differently. 4. Prepositions • Common errors: • Misuse or omission: "I am good in English." → should be "good at" • Confusion with time/place expressions: "in Monday" instead of "on Monday" • Why it matters: English prepositions are idiomatic and inconsistent. 5. Pronunciation and Intonation • Common errors: • Problems with specific sounds: /θ/ and /ð/ (“think” and “this”), or final consonants • Intonation in questions vs. statements • Why it matters: Pronunciation affects intelligibility even if grammar is perfect. 6. Vocabulary Use and Collocations • Common errors: • Word choice: "make a homework" instead of "do homework" • False friends: e.g., Spanish “embarazada” (pregnant) ≠ “embarrassed” • Why it matters: Collocations and context-specific vocabulary are often non-intuitive. 7. Plural and Countability Errors • Common errors: • Incorrect plural forms: "informations", "furnitures" • Misusing countable/uncountable nouns: "too much apples" • Why it matters: Not all languages make the same countability distinctions. 8. Negation and Question Formation • Common errors: • Incorrect negation: "He don't like it." • Wrong auxiliary use in questions: "Why you are here?" • Why it matters: English requires auxiliaries (do/does/did) and word order changes that may not exist in L1. 9. Subject-Verb Agreement • Common errors: • "The people is nice." • "There is many reasons." • Why it matters: Learners often confuse collective nouns or irregular subject structures. Rough Order of Common Concern in EFL Students (from beginner to intermediate levels, generally) 1. Verb conjugation and tense usage (Essential for clear communication of time and actions) 2. Sentence structure and word order (Affects overall intelligibility) 3. Subject-verb agreement (Frequent issue, especially early on) 4. Articles (a/an/the) (High frequency, hard to master due to lack of rules) 5. Prepositions (Very error-prone due to idiomatic usage) 6. Vocabulary and collocations (Becomes more relevant at intermediate+ levels) 7. Plural and countability (Affects clarity, especially in academic or precise speech) 8. Negation and question formation (Foundational but often resolved with more exposure) 9.
